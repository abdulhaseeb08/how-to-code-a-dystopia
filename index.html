the screen that watches you while you watch it

TELESCREEN

SAY: This is the telescreen — the machine at the centre of it all. A screen on your wall you can never switch off, broadcasting to you and watching you at the very same time. You can't tell when it's recording. So you behave as if it always is.

the device that watches back

"You had to live — did live, from habit that became instinct — in the assumption that every sound you made was overheard, and, except in darkness, every movement scrutinized." Nineteen Eighty-Four

SAY: The tool that does the watching is called the telescreen. Picture a TV that can't be switched off — and it's watching you while you watch it. The genius isn't the camera. It's that you never know, in any given second, whether a human is actually on the other end. So you assume one always is. You start policing yourself. Listen to how Orwell puts it — "from habit that became instinct." That's the whole trick: the watching stops being something done to you, and becomes something you do to yourself.

What is a thoughtcrime?

SAY: A thoughtcrime is exactly what it sounds like. The crime isn't an action — it's a thought. You don't have to do anything. Which raises the obvious question: how do you punish a thought? Who could possibly police that?

SAY: Story one — the question I get asked most: is my phone listening to me? In 2024 it looked like a yes. A marketing company's secret pitch deck leaked, advertising a product literally called "Active Listening" — serve ads based on what your phone's microphone overhears — and it named Google, Amazon and Facebook. The internet went: I knew it! But here's the twist, and it's more important than the myth. This May — three weeks ago — the FTC settled the case for nine hundred and thirty thousand dollars. And the finding was that the product never actually listened to a single conversation. There was no hot mic. What they were really doing was buying lists about you from data brokers and reselling them with a microphone-flavoured sticker on the box. Sit with that, because it's scarier than a hot mic. They didn't need to hear you. The data they already held — where you go, what you buy, who you know — said enough. A hot mic you could at least put tape over. This you can't.

THE GOSPEL picked the buildings.

SAY: Story three is the heaviest, and I'm going to tell it carefully. In Gaza, according to a major investigation by the Israeli outlet +972 Magazine, backed up by the Guardian, the military used an AI system called Lavender. At its core it's the simplest thing in machine learning — a classifier with a dial. They fed it data on known Hamas and Islamic Jihad operatives — photos, phone patterns, who they talked to, their social connections — and it learned the "characteristics" of an operative. Then it scored almost everyone in Gaza from one to a hundred: how likely the machine thinks you are a militant. Turn the dial up, and more people cross the line into "target." Now look at the features it reportedly used, because this is the part I can't unsee: being in a WhatsApp group with a known militant. Changing your phone every few months. Moving house repeatedly. Sit with that last one. Changing your number and moving house again and again is exactly what an innocent civilian does when they're displaced and fleeing bombs. The model was, in part, scoring survival as guilt. It flagged around thirty-seven thousand people. Officers told reporters the human "check" before a strike was often about twenty seconds — just long enough to confirm the target was a man. They knew going in that roughly one in ten were not militants at all. A second system, grimly named The Gospel, picked the buildings. This is the work of the people who built it, described in their own words to reporters. That is the Thought Police — not a man in a coat, a classifier with a threshold.

SAY: So Winston does the most dangerous thing he can think of. He buys a diary — an ordinary blank notebook — from a junk shop. MINISTRY OF TRUTH — where the past gets rewritten daily
 
Big Brother predicted X yesterday · Y happened instead
→ edit the record so he was right all along
an enemy becomes a friend
→ delete every old mention
a person falls out of favour
→ they were never born.
 
the old version drops into a "memory hole" and burns. there is no undo.

SAY: Winston works at the Ministry of Truth — and the joke is in the name, because its job is the opposite of truth. All day, Winston edits the past. If Big Brother promised something yesterday that didn't come true, Winston rewrites yesterday's newspaper so the promise matches reality. If a country that was the enemy becomes an ally, every record that ever called them an enemy is found and changed. If a person annoys the Party, they don't just disappear — they're edited out of history as if they were never born. The originals get dropped down a chute called a "memory hole" and incinerated. There is no undo. There is no original. And here's the thing — this isn't only fiction. We do a gentler version of it, and a Pakistani historian caught us doing it.

"Who controls the past controls the future: who controls the present controls the past." The Party · Nineteen Eighty-Four

SAY: And that's the second great slogan of the Party. Who controls the past controls the future; who controls the present controls the past. If you can rewrite what happened, you control what people believe is possible.

K.K. Aziz, The Murder of History (1985) — a respected Pakistani historian reviewed 66 textbooks, taught here from grade 1 to 14.

his finding: "What is being taught as History is really national mythology… a carefully selected collection of falsehoods, fairy tales and plain lies."

SAY: K.K. Aziz was a distinguished historian — not a foreigner, not an outsider, one of ours. In 1985 he sat down and read sixty-six of the textbooks taught in our own schools and colleges, from grade one all the way to fourteen. A Pakistani scholar, about Pakistani classrooms. Every nation does this to some degree — but we don't have to look abroad for an example. We can check it against the books our own children carry.

We celebrate the 14th.

The Indian Independence Act named 15 August 1947. Jinnah's first message called 15 August "the birthday of Pakistan." Our first postage stamps, 1948, read "15th August 1947." The legal moment was midnight, 14/15. Over the years we settled on the 14th — and now we teach the 14th, full stop.

SAY: Here's one that lives in every one of us. We celebrate Independence Day on the fourteenth of August. But the law that created Pakistan — the Indian Independence Act — named the fifteenth. Jinnah's very first message to the nation called the fifteenth of August "the birthday of the independent, sovereign State of Pakistan." Our own first postage stamps, printed in 1948, say "15th August 1947." The actual legal moment was midnight, between the 14th and the 15th. In the years that followed we settled on the fourteenth — for a mix of reasons, partly that Mountbatten held our ceremony in Karachi on the 14th before flying to Delhi for India's on the 15th, partly to mark our own distinct identity. I'm not here to tell you which date is "right." I'm telling you that the date drifted, the records were quietly reconciled, and now we teach one version to our children without the footnote. That's "who controls the past" — not with a memory hole. Just with a textbook.

But people remember.

In the book, the Party swears the chocolate ration won't be cut. Then it cuts it — from 30 grams to 20. The next day it announces the ration has been raised to 20 grams, and there are street celebrations to thank Big Brother. And people cheer. They were there yesterday. They held the 30.

RATION · 30g → 20g ✓ raised — thank big brother

SAY: Here's the catch with rewriting the past: people lived through the old version. There's a perfect little example in the book. The Party promises the chocolate ration won't be cut. Then it cuts it — from thirty grams a week down to twenty. And the very next day, the Party announces it has raised the chocolate ration to twenty grams — and there are actual celebrations in the street to thank Big Brother for his generosity. And people are grateful. Genuinely. But they were alive yesterday. They had thirty grams in their hand. How do you celebrate getting less as if it were a gift — and truly mean it, not just fake a smile? You would need a very specific mental skill. The Party built one, and gave it a name: doublethink.

Chocolate ration cut to 20g while "last week the ration had been thirty grammes"; "demonstrations to thank Big Brother for raising the chocolate ration to twenty grammes a week" — 1984, Part 1, Ch. 5.

"To know and not to know… to forget whatever it was necessary to forget, then to draw it back into memory again at the moment when it was needed, and then promptly to forget it again: and above all, to apply the same process to the process itself… consciously to induce unconsciousness, and then, once again, to become unconscious of the act of hypnosis you had just performed." Nineteen Eighty-Four

SAY: Stay with me — line by line, because this is the part that bends your brain. "To know and not to know." You hold the truth and the lie at the same time. "To forget whatever it was necessary to forget" — you push the inconvenient memory down, out of sight. "Then to draw it back into memory again at the moment when it was needed" — you can pull it up if you ever have to. "And then promptly to forget it again." And now the masterstroke — "and above all, to apply the same process to the process itself." Read that twice. You don't just forget the truth. You forget that you forgot. And then you forget that. Orwell's own words: consciously to induce unconsciousness, and then, once again, to become unconscious of the act of hypnosis you had just performed.

Doublethink definition — 1984, Part 1, Ch. 3.

DOUBLETHINK

to hold two opposite beliefs at the same time — to know they contradict — and to believe both, completely.

"In the end the Party would announce that two and two made five, and you would have to believe it." Nineteen Eighty-Four

2 + 2 = 45 accepted

SAY: Doublethink is the power to hold two flatly contradictory beliefs in your head at the same time — to know they cancel each other out — and to sincerely believe both of them anyway. Orwell's cleanest version: in the end the Party would announce that two and two made five, and you would have to believe it. Not nod along. Not pretend. Believe it. Now — that already sounds impossible. But Orwell takes it one step further, into something genuinely uncanny, and I want to read you his actual description, slowly, because this is the engine the whole machine runs on.

"two and two made five" — 1984, Part 1, Ch. 7 (diary; past tense "made").

Newspeak

"Don't you see that the whole aim of Newspeak is to narrow the range of thought? In the end we shall make thoughtcrime literally impossible, because there will be no words in which to express it." Syme · Nineteen Eighty-Four

SAY: Rewriting the past wasn't the Party's only tool. The deepest one was the language itself. They invented Newspeak — and every year it had fewer words, on purpose. A character named Syme, who builds the dictionary and is proud of it, explains the goal: narrow the range of thought, until thoughtcrime is literally impossible — because there'll be no words left to think it in. If you delete the word "freedom," eventually you delete the idea. You can't rebel for a thing you have no word for.

1984, Part 1, Ch. 5 (Syme).

"Orthodoxy means not thinking — not needing to think. Orthodoxy is unconsciousness."

"Until they become conscious they will never rebel, and until after they have rebelled they cannot become conscious." Nineteen Eighty-Four

SAY: Two lines that go together. Syme: orthodoxy means not thinking — not even needing to think. Orthodoxy is unconsciousness. The ideal Party member is asleep — agreeing on reflex. And Winston's own diary, about the masses: until they become conscious they will never rebel, and until after they have rebelled they cannot become conscious. It's a trap with no door. You can't wake up enough to fight, until you've already fought. Shrinking the language keeps everyone safely asleep.

"…rubbishy newspapers containing almost nothing except sport, crime and astrology… and sentimental songs which were composed entirely by mechanical means on a special kind of kaleidoscope known as a versificator." Nineteen Eighty-Four

machine-made content, at infinite scale, to fill the silence where a thought might go.

SAY: And here's the one that'll make you think Orwell had a time machine. In the book there's a machine called the versificator. Its whole job is to mass-produce entertainment for the masses — the newspapers full of nothing but sport and crime and horoscopes, the cheap novels, the pop songs — all churned out by machine, with no human involved. He invented AI slop in 1949. And he understood why you'd want it: if you flood people with endless, frictionless, machine-made content, you keep them pacified without having to ban a single thing. You don't censor the good stuff. You just bury it. And we built it — as of late 2024, more new articles online are written by AI than by humans. The point of the versificator was never the slop itself. It's that the slop makes the human thing — a real song, a real essay, a real argument — impossible to find, and eventually impossible to afford to make.

Ownlife

"…to do anything that suggested a taste for solitude, even to go for a walk by yourself, was always slightly dangerous. There was a word for it in Newspeak: ownlife." Nineteen Eighty-Four

SAY: So put it together: you're watched constantly, you can't trust your own thoughts, today's truth is tomorrow's lie, and the words you'd need to object are being deleted. What's left for the Party to control? One last thing — you. The fact that there's a private, separate you at all. Newspeak even has a word for the crime of being an individual: ownlife. Wanting time alone. Taking a walk by yourself. Having your own taste. That was dangerous — because a person with an inner life is a person who might want something different.
